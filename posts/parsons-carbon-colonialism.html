Carbon Colonialism: Wenn reiche Länder Klimarisiken exportieren

22. Februar 2026  ·  Philipp Schöneberg

Laurie Parsons zeigt in ihrem 2025 erschienenen Buch, wie wohlhabende Länder ihre Klimabelastungen systematisch in den globalen Süden verlagern. Eine wichtige Ergänzung zur Debatte über Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichten.

Carbon accounting and carbon colonialism

Carbon accounting by rich countries incentivizes imports insofar as they allow for emissions to be outsourced.

(Parsons, 2025, page 92)

Using the nation state as a frame of reference, rich countries effectively manage to entrench historical inequalities.

Rich nations can move their externalities across borders and out of sight.

This is not a technicality of accounting, but a significant issue.

(Parsons, 2025, p. 93)

Climate risk is also transferred to the global South

Definition of carbon colonialism: “frameworks that legitimate the exploitation of one environment for the benefit of another are colonial.”

On a practical level, this is done by transferring the emissions from one balance sheet to another.

(Parsons, 2025, page 94)

Chapter 2: Founding the global factory: the first five hundred years

Extracts must first be extracted from society (link to Patterson’s Social Death?)

(Parsons, 2025, p. 27)

Cobalt mining in DR Congo: male, often children, bonded into dangerous, toxic and often lethal work for years at a time to provide minerals needed for consumer electronics

Abuses of environments and its peoples often goes hand in hand

Extraction does not follow the economies of scale which is one of the reasons why former colonial countries have not been able to generate economics growth equal to the centers

(Parsons, 2025, p. 28)

Example of Cambodia fieldwork: Why is it that people work under the abhorrent conditions of industrial labor? Why don’t they resort to agriculture? The answer: even small farms are integrated into the international market and require capital to function. Traditional agricultural methods have stopped working in a world of increasing climate change. Farmers have to invest in products like fertilizers, fast-growing seeds, pesticide, irrigation and so on. In order to gain the necessary capital, families send their members to work in industrial facilities.

(Parsons, 2025, page 38)

Then there are reinforcing dynamics: more farmers leave the farms which increases the cost of rural labor. Once the soil is exhausted from chemical fertilizers, farmers have to invest anew. Traditional farming methods are too time consuming.

(Parsons, 2025, page 39)

Sustainability is local: “anathema to the extractive logic of a globalised world.”

(Parsons, 2025, page 46)

Environmental degradation is not a by-product, but the product.

(Parsons, 2025, page 47)

Chapter 3: Consumer power in the global factory: a lucrative illusion

“Problems are resolved by reducing their visibility” (Parsons, 2025, p. 52)

The effect of supply chain acts like the Modern Slavery Act of 2015 in the UK is limited by length, complexity and obscurity in supply chains. (Parsons, 2025, page 53)

Consumer power is not the solution to a more sustainable global economy (Parsons, 2025, page 58)

Rise of the global factory: railways, standardised stackable containers, deregulation of the global economy (1970s, 1980s, led mainly by China) made it possible for factories to become global (Parsons, 2025, page 64-65)

The global factory is characterized by product components being move via standardised containers in a way that eludes transparency.

“Fast-moving, standardised invisibility is a key obstacle to genuine accountability in supply chains.” (Parsons, 2025, page 65)

Chapter 4: Carbon colonialism: hidden emissions in the global periphery

National carbon accounting hides an important reality: emissions are simply outsourced from the global North to the global South (Parsons, 2025, page 81-82)

“carbon colonialism” is the name of the process by which more affluent countries outsource emissions to less affluent countries. (Parsons, 2025, page 82)

The analytical legacy of nationalism hides the environmental degradation that takes place in the global South on behalf of the global North (Parsons, 2025, page 83)

Chapter 5: Climate precarity

The personal experience of climate change is a question of status and financial means. (Parsons, 2025, page 104)

The wealthier, the less immediate experience of environment

(Parsons, 2025, page 105)

Climate is both a scientific and a cultural concept (“Der April macht, was er will.”)

(Parsons, 2025, page 111)

Events can never be definitively linked to climate change. The only thing that can be proven is increased likelihood of extreme weather events.

(Parsons, 2025, page 116-117)

Chapter 6: Money talks

The logic of extractivism is made to seem inescapable.

Parsons refers to Fanon stating that whoever controls the language, exerts power over what can be thought and said.

(Parsons, 2025, p. 149)

Power dynamics between people and corporations need to be recalibrated.

New environmental laws “are the most powerful weapon in the armoury of environmental defenders, capable of cutting across impossibly unequal environmental odds with the backing of leading nations and institutions.”

(Parsons, 2025, p. 150)

Environmental legislation offers a way for marginalized people to push back against the systems of production that cause the degradation of their environments.

Chapter 8: Six myths that fuel carbon colonialism

1) Climate change is causing more natural disasters

Disasters are not natural in the first place. Hazards meet vulnerability. And vulnerability is an outcome of the way the global economy functions. Natural disasters are economic disasters.

(Parsons, 2025, page 183-184)

Responsible production starts with laws and regulations

(Parsons, 2025, p. 205)

Carbon colonialism extracts value from the margins without moving capital there which leaves workers and communities with little to protect themselves against climate change

(Parsons, 2025, p. 206)

“The lack of legal frameworks governing global supply chains means the processes and practices that underpin our lives are in effect owned by no one, responsibility passed up and down the supply chain ad infinitum.”

Our lack of control and visibility over production processes is the key issue

(Parsons, 2025, p. 207)

Corporate Responsibility

Gap’s supply chain was observed from 2010 to 2016 only to realise that there is no correlation between the imposition of voluntary frameworks and subsequent improvement of standards. (Parsons, 2025, page 70)

Even if factories comply with standards, oftentimes, orders are subcontracted without authorisation. The shadow industry of subcontracting is unregulated. (Parsons, 2025, page 70)

“The contemporary reality of supply chains is that they are so knotted and impenetrable that the very companies that use them cannot elucidate, let alone reshape them to their own ends.” (Parsons, 2025, page 72)

Degrowth

Auch heute noch gehen ökonomisches Wachstum und die Degradierung der Umwelt Hand in Hand.

(Parsons, 2025, page 17)

Garment industry

5 biggest importers (H&M, Zalando, C&A, Inditex,About You) employ 5,235 factories in 60 different countries

garment industry accounts for 5-10% of global emissions

(Parsons, 2025, p. 84)

China is the world’s largest producer of cotton (20% of global output)

84% of Chinese production comes from the Xinjiang region where there were humanitarian concerns of late.

The supply chain is simply to complex to be transparent

(Parsons, 2025, p. 85)

The complexity of the supply chains also creates a great risk for carbon accountability.

(Parsons, 2025, page 87)

What are the unknown unknowns when it comes to carbon in the garment industry?

20% of black carbon is attributable to brick kilns (Parsons, 2025, page 89)

(Parsons, 2025, page 88)

Logic of colonialism

Natural resources are extracted, exported and profited from far away from the people they used to belong to (Parsons, 2025, page 6)

The relevance of distance

Distance and the practical logistics of geography still matter: they shape what is known and how well it is known, they shape the questions that are asked.(Parsons, 2025, p. 15)

There is no inspection body with a jurisdiction as vast as an international supply chain.

(Parsons, 2025, p. 96)
